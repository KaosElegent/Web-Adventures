A Silent Voice (Anime Movie)

While a large percentage of the population might've heard about the word 'anime', most of them don't know much about them (and definitely not about the movies) other than the fact that they might be cartoons. Japanese Animation, also known as anime hand-drawn and computer-generated animation originating from Japan. It has been in the general spotlight for a few decades now but the Western world was taken by surprise when anime movies started hitting the U.S. box office.

Introduction

Among them, one such hit was the Japanese romantic drama A Silent Voice, released on September 17, 2016. This movie wasn't just your run-of-the-mill anime romance though. The movie imparted several important life messages and touched on social issues. A few of these include:

  • Bullying
  • Mental Health
  • Redemption Repentance

The general plot revolves around the main character Shoyo Ishida who tries to rekindle his friendship with a deaf girl whom he bullied back in middle school. It also covers important aspects of friendships and misunderstanding, portraying them by showing us how his relationships with the people around him develop.

Consequences

In middle school, everything was going normally for Ishida until the day when a deaf girl named Shoyo Nishimiya decided to join his class. Ishida, his friends and even their other classmates bullied the new girl over the span of weeks. The consequences of this though were only faced by Ishida. His friends and the other witnesses turned their back on him and testified that Ishida was the only one who took part in the bullying and no other kids were involved. This is when everything took a turn in Ishida's life.

The bullying that occurred to Nishimiya didn't just affect her but also Ishida and the other witnesses. When Nishimiya decided to leave the school after repeated incidents, everyone started to feel the guilt set in and decided to put the blame all on Ishida. The others are in denial that they ever took part in the bullying as this would lead to a confrontation with their morality, while over the next few years, Ishida slowly becomes an outcast. This exclusion and betrayal leads to Ishida's depression.

Depression

A heartbreaking scene that I still remember from the movie even after years is the confrontation between Ishida and his mother when she realizes that her son is about to commit suicide. You can feel the emotion in every word that she says. This scene was even more touching because around the time that I saw the movie I had to confront a person close to me who was planning to end their life. This movie doesn't exaggerate any emotions or interactions, rather it depicts them in such a vivid manner that you feel involved regardless of the relatability factor.

The plot expresses several overlooked aspects of real depression. It does a great job of expressing the signs and results of depression. In Ishida's case, even years after the incident, the guilt eats away at him and this results in him completely giving up on life. It depicts the character's development from thinking that suicide is the only way out to realizing that giving life another try and confronting his past is the better option.

The following is an excerpt from 'Episode 91: A Silent Voice' of the podcast Weeb and Noob Watch Anime where Kyle and Garrett express their opinion regarding why A Silent Voice has a well written message.
"Everybody feels like they are a burden to others... everyone feels like they're at fault or blames themself for stuff. And it takes a lot of empathy and/or willingless to go outside of your own head to try to understand someone else."
-Kyle, Weeb and Noob Watch Anime Podcast

Moving On

He decides to live life so he can 'bear his sins and be punished for them'. Throughout high school, he makes no friends and sticks to his solitary lifestyle. This phase of his life is a depiction of guilt and anxiety. This social anxiety is shown in the movie with giant purple crosses seen on the faces of those around him. It also expresses his distrust in those around him. For Ishida, everyone is now a stranger who can hate him, hurt him and/or betray him.

As Ishida battles with his anxiety and attempts at friendships, he meets Nagatsuka, who becomes the first person to lose the purple cross. This indicates that Ishida is still capable of opening up and becoming comfortable around others. In the words of his new friend Nagatsuka, Friendship lies somewhere beyond things like words and logic. This sentence is further highlighted as later in the movie the viewer realizes that it's not exactly logical for Nishimiya, someone who was bullied, to become friends with her bully.

This movie gives us a different perspective on friendships. As he further explores his journey of rekindling his old friendships, he starts to question his happiness. In fact, over the plot of the movie, Ishida meets Nishimiya and they decide to become friends. But, somewhere in him resides that guilt from middle school which doesn't let him fully enjoy the newfound happiness of true friendship. He still feels as if he needs to be punished and suffer for his sins, and that he doesn't deserve to be happy with his friends.

This is only obvious after having seen the movie multiple times but, throughout the plot, Ishida was never seeking redemption. In reality, he wanted to be punished. It was repentance that he wanted. He still harboured guilt from middle school and understood the lifelong consequences of his actions on Nishimiya. This is yet another turning point in the movie.

Redemption vs Repentance

Ishida is no longer certain whether he is being Nishimiya's friend to make up for his actions in the past in an attempt to redeem himself, or whether he truly wants to be her friend and finds comfort in her.

TBH, summarising the ending of the plot I believe would ruin the experience. Instead, I wish for the readers of this article to watch the movie themselves. Eiga Koe No Katachi, translated as The Shape of Voice is the original Japanese title of the movie. It is a tale of repentance and not redemption, engulfed in a subtle layer of youthful romance. Based on a manga series by Yoshitoki Ōima, I highly recommend this movie to not just fans of anime, but to a general audience looking to broaden their view on life.
